Lesson 1: Basic Spike Dynamics

Overview

Neural networks are built from individual neurons that integrate electrical signals and fire action potentials (spikes) when certain conditions are met. This lesson explores the fundamental dynamics of single neuron behavior.

The Integrate-and-Fire Model

Each neuron in our simulation follows the leaky integrate-and-fire model, which captures the essential dynamics of real neurons:

  • Integration: The neuron accumulates incoming current over time, gradually increasing its membrane voltage
  • Threshold: When voltage reaches v=1.0, the neuron fires a spike (action potential)
  • Reset: After firing, voltage immediately resets to v=0.0
  • Leak: Voltage naturally decays toward rest without input, preventing indefinite accumulation

Mathematical Model

The mathematical model for each neuron follows the differential equation:

τ dV/dt = -V + I_input + I_noise + I_syn

Where:

  • τ (tau): Time constant controlling how fast voltage changes
  • V: Membrane voltage of the neuron
  • I_input: External input current (controlled by your slider)
  • I_noise: Random background noise
  • I_syn: Synaptic input from other neurons

Experiment Steps

Use the Input Current slider to see how external stimulation affects firing rates:

  1. Low current (0.0-0.3): Sparse, irregular firing - neurons rarely reach threshold due to insufficient input
  2. Medium current (0.4-0.8): Regular firing patterns emerge - predictable spike timing with consistent intervals
  3. High current (0.9+): High-frequency bursting - rapid continuous firing as threshold is quickly reached

What to Observe

  • Visual feedback: Watch how individual neurons (colored spheres) brighten when they spike
  • Voltage trace: Observe the voltage trace in the upper-right panel showing membrane potential over time
  • Input-output relationship: Notice the relationship between input strength and firing frequency
  • Noise effects: See how background noise affects spike timing precision and introduces variability
  • Cluster behavior: Different colored clusters represent different neuron populations

Biological Relevance

Real neurons in the brain work similarly to this model:

  • They integrate synaptic inputs from thousands of other neurons
  • They fire when a threshold is reached (typically around -55mV in biology)
  • They have refractory periods where they cannot fire again immediately
  • This simple model captures the essential dynamics of neural computation

Advanced Concepts

This basic model forms the foundation for understanding:

  • Rate coding: Information encoded in firing frequency
  • Temporal coding: Information encoded in precise spike timing
  • Neural oscillations: Rhythmic patterns of activity
  • Synchronization: Coordinated firing across neuron populations
