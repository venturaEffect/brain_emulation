Lesson 2: Synaptic Transmission

How Neurons Communicate

Neurons don't work in isolation - they form complex networks through synaptic connections that transmit signals between cells. This lesson explores how network connectivity shapes brain function and emergent behavior.

Synaptic Weights

The strength of connection between neurons is controlled by synaptic weight. This determines how much influence one neuron has on another:

  • Weak synapses (0.0-0.2): Subtle influence, requires many simultaneous spikes to significantly affect the target neuron
  • Medium synapses (0.3-0.6): Moderate influence, single spikes can noticeably change target voltage
  • Strong synapses (0.7+): Powerful influence, can trigger immediate firing in target neuron

Network Connectivity

The Connection Probability determines how densely neurons are wired together:

  • Sparse connectivity (0.02-0.05): Isolated activity, signals don't spread far through the network
  • Medium connectivity (0.06-0.12): Activity spreads through network in waves and cascades
  • Dense connectivity (0.13+): Synchronized network-wide activity, all neurons tend to fire together

Types of Synapses

  • Excitatory synapses: Increase target neuron's voltage (push toward firing threshold)
  • Inhibitory synapses: Decrease target neuron's voltage (prevent firing, provide control)
  • Chemical synapses: Use neurotransmitters to transmit signals (what we simulate)
  • Electrical synapses: Direct electrical coupling between neurons (gap junctions)

Network Dynamics

When neurons are connected, complex dynamics emerge:

  • Propagation: Spikes travel through connected pathways
  • Amplification: Single spikes can trigger cascades of activity
  • Competition: Excitation and inhibition balance each other
  • Oscillations: Rhythmic patterns of network activity

Experiment Protocol

  1. Start with low synaptic weight (0.1) and sparse connectivity (0.03)
  2. Gradually increase synaptic weight - observe how activity spreads further
  3. Reset and try increasing connectivity instead - compare the effects
  4. Find the "critical point" where isolated spikes become network-wide bursts
  5. Try different combinations to see emergent behaviors

What to Observe

Pay attention to these network phenomena:

  • Signal propagation: How spikes travel through connected neurons
  • Activity cascades: Single spikes triggering multiple downstream spikes
  • Cluster synchronization: Groups of neurons firing together
  • Network bursts: Periods of high activity across many neurons
  • Connection visualization: Colored lines showing synaptic links

Real Brain Networks

In the real brain:

  • Each neuron receives ~7,000 synaptic inputs on average
  • There are ~86 billion neurons in the human brain
  • Network connectivity follows specific anatomical patterns
  • Different brain regions have different connectivity profiles
  • Synaptic strength can change with experience (plasticity)

Clinical Relevance

Understanding synaptic transmission is crucial for:

  • Epilepsy: Excessive synchronization and network bursts
  • Depression: Altered neurotransmitter function
  • Schizophrenia: Disrupted network connectivity
  • Alzheimer's: Loss of synaptic connections
