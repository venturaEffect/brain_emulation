Lesson 3: Network Plasticity

Learning and Adaptation

Real neural networks aren't static - they continuously change their connectivity based on experience through synaptic plasticity. This is the biological foundation of learning, memory, and adaptation.

Hebbian Learning Principle

The famous principle "neurons that fire together, wire together" describes the fundamental mechanism of synaptic plasticity:

  • Correlation detection: When pre- and post-synaptic neurons are active simultaneously
  • Synaptic strengthening: The synapse between them becomes stronger (increased weight)
  • Activity-dependent: Strengthening depends on correlated neural activity
  • Associative learning: Forms the basis for associating related inputs

Types of Synaptic Plasticity

  • Long-Term Potentiation (LTP): Persistent strengthening of synapses lasting hours to days
  • Long-Term Depression (LTD): Weakening of synapses, important for forgetting
  • Spike-Timing Dependent Plasticity (STDP): Timing of pre/post spikes determines direction of change
  • Homeostatic plasticity: Global mechanisms that maintain network stability
  • Metaplasticity: Plasticity of plasticity itself - learning how to learn

Network Reorganization

As synapses change strength over time, the network's behavior evolves:

  • Pathway formation: Frequently used connections become "superhighways" for information
  • Attractor states: Network develops preferred patterns of activity
  • Memory consolidation: Important patterns become more stable over time
  • Synaptic pruning: Unused connections weaken and may be eliminated
  • Critical periods: Times when plasticity is especially high

Mechanisms of Plasticity

At the cellular level, plasticity involves:

  • Neurotransmitter release: Changes in probability and amount of transmitter release
  • Receptor modifications: Changes in number and sensitivity of postsynaptic receptors
  • Structural changes: Growth of new dendritic spines and synaptic connections
  • Gene expression: Activity-dependent changes in protein synthesis

Learning Rules

Different mathematical rules capture various aspects of plasticity:

  • Hebb's rule: Δw = η × pre × post
  • BCM rule: Includes sliding threshold for stability
  • STDP rule: Weight change depends on spike timing
  • Oja's rule: Normalized Hebbian learning

Experimental Procedure

  1. Reset the network to start with random connectivity
  2. Observe initial activity patterns with default settings
  3. Run for extended time and watch how patterns evolve and stabilize
  4. Use "Show Weights" to reveal current synaptic strengths
  5. Notice which connections have strengthened vs. weakened over time
  6. Try injecting specific patterns and see how they modify the network

What to Observe

  • Weight evolution: How synaptic strengths change over time
  • Pattern emergence: Development of preferred activity patterns
  • Stability vs. plasticity: Balance between learning and maintaining memories
  • Competitive dynamics: Stronger connections getting stronger (rich-get-richer)
  • Network efficiency: Development of more efficient connectivity patterns

Biological Significance

Plasticity underlies many cognitive functions:

  • Learning and memory: Formation and storage of new information
  • Development: Refinement of neural circuits during growth
  • Recovery: Compensation after brain injury
  • Adaptation: Adjusting to environmental changes
  • Skill acquisition: Learning motor and cognitive skills

Disorders of Plasticity

When plasticity goes wrong:

  • Alzheimer's disease: Loss of synaptic connections and plasticity
  • Autism: Altered synaptic development and plasticity
  • Addiction: Pathological plasticity in reward circuits
  • PTSD: Maladaptive memory formation
  • Depression: Reduced plasticity and network flexibility

Try This

  • Can you train the network to prefer certain activity patterns?
  • What happens when you repeatedly stimulate the same neurons?
  • How long do plastic changes persist in the network?
  • Can you create "memories" that interfere with each other?
