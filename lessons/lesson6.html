Excitation vs. Inhibition

Two Types of Neurons

Real brains contain two fundamental types of neurons:

  • Excitatory neurons (~80%): Increase target neuron's voltage (positive weights)
  • Inhibitory neurons (~20%): Decrease target neuron's voltage (negative weights)

Winner-Take-All Dynamics

Inhibitory connections create competition between neurons:

  • Lateral inhibition: Active neurons suppress their neighbors
  • Global inhibition: Overall network activity is regulated
  • Selection: Only the strongest signals survive
  • Amplification: Small differences become large decisions

üí° Key Insight

The balance between excitation and inhibition (E/I balance) is crucial for stable, selective neural computation. Too much excitation leads to seizures; too much inhibition leads to silence.

Competitive Mechanisms

Attention and Selection

Competitive networks enable:

  • Attentional focus: Selecting relevant stimuli while ignoring distractors
  • Decision making: Choosing between competing actions
  • Pattern completion: Filling in missing information
  • Noise reduction: Filtering out weak or inconsistent signals

  4. Note how threshold affects the strength of competition

Real-World Applications

Brain Functions Requiring Competition

  • Visual attention: Focusing on one object while ignoring others
  • Motor control: Selecting one movement while inhibiting alternatives
  • Language: Choosing words from vocabulary during speech
  • Memory retrieval: Accessing specific memories from vast storage

Disorders of Competition

  • ADHD: Impaired attentional competition and selection
  • Parkinson's: Reduced ability to select and initiate movements
  • Schizophrenia: Disrupted competitive filtering of sensory information
  • Epilepsy: Loss of inhibitory control leading to runaway excitation

Mathematical Foundation

üìä Competitive Dynamics

In winner-take-all networks:

  • Input strength: Determines initial advantage
  • Lateral inhibition: Amplifies differences between competitors
  • Threshold nonlinearity: Creates sharp selection boundaries
  • Feedback loops: Maintain stable winner states

Implications for Brain Emulation

Engineering Challenges

  • Precise E/I balance: Must be maintained across brain regions
  • Dynamic inhibition: Inhibitory strength must adapt to context
  • Multi-scale competition: Selection occurs at multiple levels simultaneously
  • Stability vs. flexibility: Decisions must be stable but reversible

üî¨ Research Note

Current brain emulation projects struggle with implementing realistic inhibition. Getting the E/I balance right across different brain regions and scales is one of the hardest challenges in whole brain emulation.

Key Takeaways

  1. Inhibition is as important as excitation for neural computation
  2. Competition creates selection and decision-making capabilities
  3. E/I balance must be carefully tuned for stable operation
  4. Many brain disorders involve disrupted competitive mechanisms
